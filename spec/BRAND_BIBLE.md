# MODULE 1 — BRAND FOUNDATION
### The Strategic Anchor

Everything downstream — every finish ring, every dealer email, every Performance Max headline — resolves back to this module. If a creative decision cannot be traced to a pillar here, it is off-brand by definition.

## 1.1 Vision

> To be the last game room furniture a family ever needs to buy — the heirloom centerpiece that outlives the mortgage, hosts three generations, and is handed down rather than hauled off.

We do not compete on the showroom floor. We compete on the **timeline of a home**. A C.L. Bailey table is engineered to be in the room in 2055.

## 1.2 Mission

To design, build, and finish solid-hardwood game room furniture — entirely free of particle board and MDF — that performs at championship standards for play and integrates as true residential furniture for living. We arm an authorized dealer network with the margin security, material proof, and brand assets to sell that promise with total confidence.

## 1.3 Core Pillars

The brand stands on three load-bearing columns. Like the legs of a slate-bed table, removing any one collapses the structure.

### Pillar I — Craftsmanship as Proof, Not Poetry
We earn the word "handcrafted" with verifiable facts: solid hardwood throughout, **1-inch diamond-honed slate**, and **8 signature hand-applied multi-coat finishes**. Craftsmanship is a spec sheet, not an adjective. Every claim must survive a tape measure and a moisture meter.

### Pillar II — Structural Endurance
We build for **residential longevity** measured in decades. The enemy is not a competitor — it is humidity, weight, leveling drift, and the landfill. Cast-iron-grade durability, kiln-dried hardwood, and re-levelable construction are the substance behind "Built for Homes That Endure."

### Pillar III — Multi-Functional Space Utilization
A C.L. Bailey piece earns its footprint twice. Game night to dinner party in one piece of furniture — **dual-purpose convertible dining/buffet tops** and **matched-family collections** (Skylar, Viking, Tunbridge) that bring complete room harmony. The **Dutchess** pool-table collection sits alongside the matched families. We sell *rooms that work*, not objects that sit.

## 1.4 Target Personas

Three people fund this brand. Every asset must speak to at least one without alienating the other two.

### Persona A — The Discerning Homeowner
- **Who:** Design-literate, 38–58, renovating or finishing a dedicated room. Reads shelter magazines; has a designer or strong opinions.
- **Core anxiety:** "Will this look like a *bar*, or like *furniture*?"
- **What they need to see:** Finish-matched collections, real interiors, fabric/wood pairings, the convertible dining top that hides the felt for a dinner party.
- **Winning message:** *Furniture first. Game table second. No compromise on either.*

### Persona B — The Legacy Family
- **Who:** Multi-generational household, 40–65, investing in a centerpiece for holidays and milestones. Buying memory, not merchandise.
- **Core anxiety:** "Is this a *purchase* or an *investment* my kids will fight over someday — in a good way?"
- **What they need to see:** Longevity proof, the Lifetime Structural Guarantee, the Owner's Care Kit, the "handed down, not hauled off" narrative.
- **Winning message:** *Buy it once. Pass it down.*

### Persona C — The Authorized Dealer Principal
- **Who:** Showroom owner, regional distributor, or floor manager whose income depends on turns and margin.
- **Core anxiety:** "Will this protect my margin, ship reliably, and not blow up in a warranty claim after I've vouched for it?"
- **What they need to see:** MSRP discipline, bulletproof material durability (the No-MDF promise as a *selling weapon*), reliable distribution, co-op ad support, and a portal that makes them look professional to *their* customer.
- **Winning message:** *Sell it with confidence. We've already won the durability argument for you.*

## 1.5 Brand Positioning Statement

> **For** discerning homeowners and the dealers who serve them, **C.L. Bailey & Co.** is the handcrafted game room furniture brand that sits at the unique intersection of **premium residential furniture design** and **championship-level play performance** — because unlike mass-market builders who hide MDF behind veneer and chase a price point, we build entirely from solid hardwood on 1-inch diamond-honed slate, finish by hand in 8 signature coats, and engineer every piece to be re-leveled, restored, and inherited. We are the table that is still in the room a generation later.

**The intersection, stated as a metric promise:** Tournament-grade flatness (1-inch diamond-honed slate, leveled to play spec) *and* dining-room-grade finish (8 hand-applied coats, furniture-matched collections) in a single, convertible piece. Most brands choose one axis. We refuse the trade-off.

# MODULE 2 — VISUAL IDENTITY
### The Creative Assets

## 2.1 Logo Hierarchy

Three marks, three jobs. They are not interchangeable. Choosing the wrong mark for the context is a brand violation, not a style preference.

### 2.1.1 Primary Mark — The Heritage Badge
- **Form:** The ornamental Victorian badge — black filigree frame, "THE C.L. BAILEY CO" lettering in **Heritage Green** (`#006840`, see §2.4.0), and the "Tomball, Texas" ribbon below. The green lettering is part of the mark; it is the *only* place Heritage Green appears in digital layouts other than the felt itself.
- **Use:** Hero placements, page 1 of any document, showroom hero signage, the home page, the cover of every catalog.
- **Rule:** The Primary Mark **MUST** appear at least once, at the largest hierarchy, on any first impression surface (cover, hero, storefront). On Deep Charcoal or wood grounds the badge sits on a Warm White plate — the mark itself is never reversed, recolored, or rebuilt.

### 2.1.2 Secondary Mark — Horizontal Lockup · **STATUS: SPECIFIED — NOT YET BUILT**
- **Form:** "C.L. BAILEY & CO." wordmark locked to the right of the crest/submark, on a single baseline, optimized for short, wide spaces.
- **Use (once built):** Website headers, email signatures, dealer co-op ad footers, document running headers, navigation bars.
- **Rule:** Use whenever vertical height is constrained below the Primary Mark's minimum (see §2.3) but horizontal room exists.

### 2.1.3 Submark / Crest — The Favicon, Embroidery & Cast Nameplate · **STATUS: SPECIFIED — NOT YET BUILT**
- **Form:** A simplified monogram crest — interlocked "CLB" or the standalone ampersand-and-rule device — designed to survive reduction to a 16px favicon, a stitched apparel emblem, and a **cast brass nameplate** mounted on the table rail.
- **Use (once built):** Favicon, app icon, apparel embroidery, social avatar, the physical brass plate on every table, hang-tag seal.
- **Rule:** The Submark **MUST** remain legible as a single-color silhouette. No gradients, no fine interior detail thinner than the stroke that survives a 12mm embroidery stitch or a 1.0mm cast relief.

### 2.1.4 Production Reality & Open Asset Actions (v2.0 audit, June 2026)

Exactly **one mark exists in production**: the Primary badge (`/images/brand/clb-logo.png` on the site; `CLB-LOGO-PrimaryMark-Full-v01.png` in the vault). The Secondary Lockup and the Crest are governed on paper but have never been drawn. Until they ship:

- **Interim rule:** the Primary badge serves *all* placements. Below the badge's 180px legibility floor, use the badge on its Warm White plate at the largest size the space allows — **never** improvise a lockup, monogram, or cropped badge to fill the gap.
- **Known violation:** the current site favicon is the full filigree badge reduced to icon sizes, which fails §2.1.3's 16px single-color silhouette rule by definition. It is tolerated only until the Crest exists.
- **Standing priority:** commissioning the **Crest** is the #1 open asset action (it unblocks favicon, avatar, embroidery, and the rail nameplate); the Lockup follows. Both require brand-team commissioning and vault release per §2.7 — no ad-hoc creation by dealers, vendors, or AI.

## 2.2 Exclusion Zone (Clear Space)

Clear space is measured in units of **"C"** — the cap height of the leading "C" in the logotype.

- **Minimum clear space on all four sides:** `1 × C` for digital, `1.5 × C` for print and physical signage.
- **Nothing** — no text, rule, image edge, fold, or trim — **MAY** intrude into the exclusion zone.
- For the Submark/Crest, the exclusion zone equals **50% of the crest's total height** on every side.

## 2.5 Typography

A four-family system, mirrored exactly from the live clbailey.com build, plus a hard-coded fallback stack for environments we don't control (dealer email clients).

### 2.5.1 Display Serif — **Le Jour Serif** (`leJourSerif`)
- **Role:** Every headline: hero, page titles, section heads, card titles, pull quotes — the "voice of the master carpenter." Also the serif of record for showroom signage and print display.
- **Weights:** **400 only.** Le Jour Serif is loaded in a single weight; it is never bolded, faux-bolded, or italicized. Hierarchy comes from size and space, not weight.
- **Rules:** Letter-spacing normal at display sizes (`.heading-sub` carries `+0.5px`). Line-height 1.05 at hero scale, 1.08 at display scale. Never set Le Jour Serif below 19px on screen — below that, switch to Raleway labels or Roboto body.
- **License:** Le Jour Serif is a licensed family served from the site; the licensed webfont files live in the vault (`02_Color_Type/`). Substitution requires approval.

### 2.5.2 Body Sans — **Roboto** (`--font-sans`)
- **Role:** Body copy, paragraphs, UI text, forms. Weight 400.
- **Rules:** Body 16px / line-height 1.55–1.65, color Mid Gray `#6B6560` on light grounds. Minimum body size 14px. Never set body in Bronze or Sand on a light ground.

### 2.5.3 Display Sans & Labels — **Roboto Condensed** & **Raleway**
- **Roboto Condensed** (`--font-display`, weights 300/400): data, spec tables, condensed subheads, and the sans fallback behind Le Jour Serif.
- **Raleway** (`--font-label`, weights 500/600): the label voice — eyebrows, navigation, buttons, and small caps annotations. Always uppercase with `+2px` to `+3px` letter-spacing.

### 2.5.4 Type Scale (from the live build)

| Token | Spec | Use |
|---|---|---|
| Hero | `clamp(40px → 72px)` · lh 1.05 | Le Jour Serif 400 · hero only |
| Display | scale to layout · lh 1.08 | Le Jour Serif 400 · page titles, Graphite |
| Subhead | 22px / 28px · ls +0.5px | Le Jour Serif 400 · Mid Gray |
| Card title | 19px / 24px | Le Jour Serif 400 · Graphite |
| Body | 16px · lh 1.6 | Roboto 400 · Mid Gray |
| Label / Button | 11px · 600 · UPPERCASE · ls +2px | Raleway |
| Micro | 11px | Legal, disclaimers · Roboto |

### 2.5.5 Shape & Surface Rules (the "almost-square" law)
- **Corner radius:** `2px` on buttons and interactive elements; `0` everywhere else. Nothing pill-shaped, nothing visibly rounded.
- **Buttons:** 1px border, `12px 28px` padding, Raleway 11px/600 uppercase `+2px` tracking. Variants: Graphite fill + white label (`btn-primary`); Cloud outline + Graphite label (`btn-outline`); translucent white outline on dark (`btn-outline-white`); Sand fill + Deep Charcoal label (`btn-sand`).
- **Shadows:** none. Depth comes from rules, surfaces, and photography overlays — never drop shadows.

### 2.5.6 Universal Fallback Stack (dealer email & uncontrolled environments)

```css
/* Headlines */
font-family: Georgia, "Times New Roman", serif;       /* stands in for Le Jour Serif */
/* Body / UI */
font-family: Roboto, "Helvetica Neue", Arial, sans-serif;
```

Dealer email templates **MUST** use the fallback stack — never web-font-only declarations — because outbound dealer mail renders in Outlook, Apple Mail, and Gmail without our hosted fonts. Le Jour Serif never ships in email; Georgia carries the serif voice there.

## 2.6 Imagery Direction

- **Always:** High-end residential photography. Warm, authentic ambient light (golden-hour, lamp-lit, hearth-lit). Real, lived-in interior spaces. Families *using* the furniture — a dinner served on the convertible top, a multi-generation game night, a quiet morning with the room at rest.
- **Never:** Clinical white-background "catalog cutout" stock. Cold studio strobe. Empty rooms with no human warmth. Over-saturated "showroom" lighting. Any image where the table reads as a *bar fixture* rather than *furniture*.
- **Composition:** Wood grain and finish must be legible. At least one image per collection must show the convertible dining top *deployed* (felt hidden) to prove the dual-purpose claim.
- **Color grade:** Warm white balance (4800–5200K feel), protected shadows, felt green and wood tones true-to-finish. Never crush blacks to pure `#000`; honor Deep Charcoal `#1C1C1E`.

## 2.8 Motion

**Heritage doesn't dart.** Motion is the room settling — never the interface performing. These tokens mirror the live build (300ms button transitions, staggered fade-up entrances) and are law for every animated brand surface: web, video overlays, digital signage.

### 2.8.1 Motion Tokens

| Token | Spec | Use |
|---|---|---|
| Micro | 200–300ms · `ease` | Hover, focus, button color shifts |
| Entrance | 400–600ms · `ease-out` | Fade-up on scroll/load, translate ≤24px |
| Stagger | 100–150ms between siblings | Sequenced entrances (hero → sub → CTA) |
| Photographic | ≥8s · linear · scale ≤105% | Slow pan/zoom in video heroes only |

### 2.8.2 Allowed / Banned

- **Allowed:** opacity fades; entrance translate-up of 24px or less; hover color transitions; slow photographic pan or scale in video heroes; one entrance animation per element per page view.
- **Banned:** parallax; scroll-jacking or scroll-driven scenes; bounce, spring, or elastic easing; hover scale beyond 1.02; autoplaying carousels faster than 6 seconds per slide; marquees; spinners or loaders styled as brand moments; infinite loops outside video. **The mark never animates** — no spinning badges, no drawing-on filigree, no shimmer.

### 2.8.3 Reduced Motion (absolute)

`prefers-reduced-motion: reduce` **MUST** be honored on every surface: entrances render instantly in their final state, video heroes show their poster frame, carousels stop auto-advancing. This is an accessibility law (§7.2's sibling), not a preference.

# MODULE 3 — EDITORIAL IDENTITY
### The Copy & Voice

## 3.1 Tone

Timeless, honest, authoritative — yet approachable, like a master carpenter explaining their work across the bench. We state facts plainly and let the material do the bragging. We never oversell, because the spec sheet already won. We respect the reader's intelligence and their money.

The voice is the voice of someone who has built the thing with their hands, knows exactly why the slate is one inch and not three-quarters, and will tell you — without condescension — what that means for the next thirty years of your home.

## 3.2 The "This, Not That" Copywriting Matrix

| We Are… | …But NOT | In Practice |
|---|---|---|
| **Premium** | Elitist | "Built to be used by your whole family," not "for the discerning few." |
| **Traditional** | Antiquated | "Time-tested joinery," not "old-fashioned." We honor heritage without smelling of mothballs. |
| **Durable** | Industrial | "Engineered to be re-leveled and inherited," not "heavy-duty contractor-grade." Furniture, not equipment. |
| **Confident** | Arrogant | "The slate is one inch. Here's why that matters," not "the best table money can buy." |
| **Warm** | Folksy/Cutesy | "Made by hand in Tomball," not "good ol' Texas craftsmanship, y'all." |
| **Detailed** | Jargon-heavy | Explain the diamond-honing; don't hide behind it. |
| **Aspirational** | Pretentious | "The room your family gathers in," not "an objet d'art for the connoisseur." |

## 3.3 Mechanical Rules — Punctuation & Numerics (Absolute)

- **Oxford comma:** REQUIRED, always. "Solid hardwood, diamond-honed slate, and hand-applied finish."
- **Exclamation points:** MAXIMUM **one (1)** per dealer announcement; **zero** in product copy, spec language, or warranty text. Enthusiasm is shown through the fact, not the punctuation.
- **Em dash:** Use the em dash (—) with spaces around it for asides. The brand voice favors the considered pause.
- **Numerals:** Spell out one through nine in prose; use figures for 10 and above, and ALWAYS use figures for measurements, prices, and specs ("1-inch slate," "8 finishes," "$4,295 MSRP"). Never "one-inch slate" in spec contexts.
- **Measurements:** Always hyphenate compound modifiers: "1-inch diamond-honed slate," "3/4-inch" only when quoting an inferior competitor spec for contrast.
- **Prices:** Always show MSRP with a dollar sign and comma separators; never round-trick ("$3,995," never "under $4k").
- **Capitalization:** Collection names are proper nouns — always capitalized (Skylar, Viking, Tunbridge, Dutchess). "Diamond-Honed Slate" is title-case as a proprietary feature term on first use, lowercase thereafter.

The same collection jumps art direction shot-to-shot: Viking is beach-house bright in one frame, industrial-loft moody in the next, desert-contemporary in a third. §2.6's intent (a coherent "lived-in home") is impossible when each image invents a new house. Collections read as *matched families* in copy and *orphans* in pictures.

**F-6. AI artifacts visible at production size.**
Spot checks show classic tells: incoherent abstract wall art, prop debris under tables, slightly waxy faces in group scenes, architecture that doesn't resolve. Each one quietly erodes the "honest joinery" promise. There is no evidence of a human QC gate (§8.3's "brand truth check") between generation and publish.

### MEDIUM

**F-7. The workshop is missing.**
"Built by hand. Guaranteed for life." is illustrated by an anonymous hands-on-wood shot. There is no visible library of the actual Tomball shop floor: saws, finish room, slate leveling, the people. The single most defensible photographic asset this brand could own — real provenance — is absent. (§2.7.2 reserves `03_Photography/` for it; the social grid in §4 requires craft/material macros.)

**F-8. Image delivery hygiene.**
Multiple slots render as blank beige for seconds (lazy-load with no LQIP placeholder); transforms request `w-3840` at `q-70` even for card-size slots; at least one URL ships with no format extension and a stray `?updatedAt` cache-buster; one filename contains ` (1)` — a desktop duplicate artifact now in production. Mixed naming (`PTB_HERO_r6IPkaQze.png` vs `skylar combo_ySLqSRsgG.jpg` — spaces in URLs) confirms there is no enforced ingest gate on the ImageKit library.

**F-9. Convertible-top proof is under-shot.**
§2.6 requires every collection to show the dining top *deployed*. Coverage is inconsistent — the strongest version is an AI dinner-party scene rather than a real conversion sequence (the one thing a photograph can prove and a render can only claim).

## A.3 What's working

To be kept and built on: the **green-walled Skylar library scenes** are the closest thing to a correct brand world — warm, lamp-lit, green-felted, furniture-not-equipment. The **moody craft/hands imagery** direction is right even if the current execution is generic. The site's typography-led design carries heritage; the photography just needs to live in the same house.

# PART B — THE PHOTOGRAPHY STANDARD

*This is the governing standard for every image on every C.L. Bailey surface: web, social, paid, dealer portal, print. If an image cannot be traced to a shot type in §10.2 and pass the gate in §10.8, it does not ship.*

## 10.1 The One-Sentence Standard

**Every C.L. Bailey photograph is a warm, lamp-lit room in one believable Texas home, where solid hardwood furniture is being lived with — and the felt is Heritage Green.**

## 10.2 The Shot Taxonomy

Six shot types. Every asset in the library is exactly one of these.

| # | Type | Code | Purpose | Notes |
|---|------|------|---------|-------|
| 1 | **Residential Hero** | `Hero` | Collection/product anchor. The room at its best hour. | One room per collection (§10.4). 16:9 web / 4:5 feed. |
| 2 | **Family-in-Use** | `Lifestyle` | People using the furniture: game night, dinner on the top, quiet morning. | Hands and posture natural; nobody posing at camera. |
| 3 | **Craft Macro** | `Macro` | Grain, joinery, slate edge, finish coat, brass nameplate. | Shallow DOF. Wood grain MUST be legible (§2.6). |
| 4 | **Conversion Demo** | `Demo` | The dining top deployed — sequence or pair (felt → dinner). | Required once per collection. Photograph, never render. |
| 5 | **Workshop / Provenance** | `Shop` | The Tomball floor: saws, finish room, leveling, the makers. | Real only. AI is banned for this type without exception. |
| 6 | **Product Study** | `Study` | Clean three-quarter and detail views on a quiet, warm ground. | Never white-seamless (§2.6 "never"). Warm White or wood-toned environment. |

Social grid rhythm (§4.1) maps to types 1/3/2. Paid asset groups (§6) require types 1, 3, and 4.

## 10.3 Light, Color, Felt

- **Light:** Warm ambient — golden-hour window light, lamps, hearth. Practical sources visible in frame where natural. Never cold strobe, never midday flat-bright, never LED-strip "games room" lighting.
- **Grade:** Warm white balance, 4800–5200K feel. Protected shadows — moody is correct, crushed is not. No pure `#000` blacks (honor Deep Charcoal `#1C1C1E`). Felt green and wood tones true-to-finish; no saturation pushes.
- **Felt:** **Heritage Green (`#006840`) — the green of the mark's lettering — is the default playing surface in all brand photography.** A second approved felt may appear only when the image's explicit subject is the felt program (Module: Felt/Velocity Pro) — never as set dressing. Burgundy, blue, purple, black, and tan felts are retired from brand imagery. This single rule does more for shelf-recognition than any logo placement.
- **Palette discipline:** Rooms are dressed inside the brand system — warm whites, sand, charcoal iron, the wood finishes, and bronze accents. The green felt is the room's single saturated note; a prop color outside the system must be incidental, never dominant.

## 10.4 One Collection, One House

Each collection lives in **one architectural world**, documented as a one-page "room bible" (location, light, wall color, flooring, props) before any shoot or generation:

- **Skylar** — *the modern green library.* Clean-lined contemporary home; deep-green millwork; brass and linen; morning-to-lamplight range. (The existing green-walled Skylar scenes are the seed — keep them.)
- **Viking** — *the Texas hill-country lodge.* Stone, timber, forged iron; big hearth; dusk and firelight. Replaces both the beach-house catalog world and the Manhattan loft world.
- **Tunbridge** — *the transitional family home.* Warm whites, weathered brown, generational mix; Sunday-afternoon light.
- **Dutchess** — *world to be defined.* No room bible exists yet; one MUST be written and approved before the next Dutchess shoot or generation ships.

Rules: no skylines that aren't Texan, no desert-modern unless a collection world is explicitly defined there, no white-box lofts. A new world requires the same written approval as a new brand color (§2.2).

## 10.5 People

Multi-generational, natural, mid-action — reaching for a cue, laying a place setting, leaning on a rail. Faces may be soft or turned; the furniture is the protagonist. Casting reflects real American homes. Nobody wears brand-clashing saturated colors. No stock-photo grins at camera.

## 10.6 AI-Generated Imagery — The Governed Policy

This section **amends §8.3** to match operational reality: C.L. Bailey uses generative imagery (Nano Banana / Gemini, or successors) in production. Denying it is untenable; governing it is mandatory.

**Tiering:**

| Tier | Surfaces | AI permitted? |
|------|----------|---------------|
| 1 | Product Studies, Craft Macros, Conversion Demos, Workshop/Provenance, co-op & paid ads making product claims | **No.** Real photography only. These are *proof* surfaces. |
| 2 | Residential Heroes, Family-in-Use lifestyle on web & social | **Yes, conditionally** — the depicted furniture must be dimensionally and materially true to the real product (built from real product photography as input, not imagined), and the image must pass the §10.8 gate. |
| 3 | Internal concepting, mood boards, room-bible exploration | Yes, freely. |

**Non-negotiables for Tier 2:**
1. **Truth of product.** Proportions, finish, hardware, pocket and leg count exactly match the shipping product. Anatomically wrong product = non-shippable (§8.3).
2. **Truth of world.** The image obeys §10.3 and §10.4 like any photograph.
3. **Human QC sign-off.** Named reviewer, recorded in the asset's metadata, before upload.
4. **Clean filenames.** Prompt text and generator names NEVER appear in any public URL, filename, alt text, or EXIF. Renamed per §10.7 *before* upload to ImageKit.
5. **No fake provenance.** AI never depicts the workshop, the makers, or "our factory." The craft story is the one place a synthetic image would be a lie rather than an illustration.

## 10.7 File Naming & ImageKit Hygiene

Extends §2.7 to the photography library. Pattern:

```
CLB-[Collection|Module]-[ShotType]-[Subject]-[Source]-vNN.[ext]
```

- **Collection:** `Skylar`, `Viking`, `Tunbridge`, `Dutchess`, `Brand` (non-collection)
- **ShotType:** `Hero`, `Lifestyle`, `Macro`, `Demo`, `Shop`, `Study`
- **Source:** `Ph` (photograph) or `Gen` (generated) — internal honesty marker, innocuous in public
- Examples: `CLB-Skylar-Hero-Library-Gen-v02.jpg` · `CLB-Viking-Demo-DiningTop-Ph-v01.jpg`

**ImageKit rules:**
- No spaces, no parentheses, no ` (1)` duplicates, no prompt text, no missing extensions.
- Folder mirror: `/CLB_Library/[Collection]/[ShotType]/`.
- Transform discipline: request the rendered size (`w-800` for cards, `w-1920`–`w-2560` for heroes — not `w-3840` everywhere); `f-auto`; `q-75` heroes / `q-80` macros (grain must survive).
- Every image slot ships a low-quality placeholder (ImageKit `q-10,bl-6` LQIP) — no more blank beige boxes.
- Monthly audit (same cadence as §2.7): non-conforming files are renamed or purged.

## 10.8 The Publish Gate

No image ships to any public or dealer surface until every box is checked:

- [ ] Maps to exactly one §10.2 shot type
- [ ] Felt is Heritage Green (or image is an approved felt-program asset)
- [ ] Light and grade meet §10.3 (warm, protected shadows, true tones)
- [ ] Setting belongs to the collection's documented world (§10.4)
- [ ] Wood grain legible; product proportions true; pockets/legs/rails anatomically correct
- [ ] If generated: Tier permitted, product-true, named human sign-off, `Gen` source tag
- [ ] Filename conforms to §10.7; no prompt text anywhere in URL, alt, or metadata
- [ ] Alt text per §7 (descriptive, collection-led; decorative gets `alt=""`)
- [ ] Correct transform size for the slot; LQIP present

## 10.9 Remediation Priorities

1. **This week — kill F-1.** Rename every `Nano_Banana` / prompt-text file in ImageKit and update site references. This is exposure, not polish.
2. **Next 30 days.** Retire off-felt and off-world imagery from primary surfaces (homepage rotation, collection heroes); standardize on the green-felt assets that already exist; write the three room bibles (§10.4); fix transforms + LQIP.
3. **Next quarter.** Commission the one shoot that AI cannot do: **two days in the Tomball workshop** (type 5) plus one real Conversion Demo sequence per collection (type 4). These become the proof layer under the generated lifestyle layer — and the only image set no competitor can copy.

# MODULE 11 — MACHINE READABILITY & AI SEARCH

A growing share of discovery now happens *inside* AI assistants — ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini, Google AI Overviews. They cite whoever states facts most clearly, consistently, and crawlably. **The consistency law:** these facts MUST be byte-identical everywhere they appear — site footer, JSON-LD, Google Business Profile, directories, dealer co-op, press boilerplate. One stale address or wrong founding year degrades machine trust in *all* the others. The vault holds the master copy (`02_Color_Type/CLB-M11-FactSheet-Full-vNN.md`); everything else mirrors it.

## 11.2 Structured Data (JSON-LD, required)

- **Organization** on every page: `name`, `legalName`, `foundingDate: "1999"`, `address`, `telephone`, `logo`, `sameAs` (the §11.1 profiles).
- **Product** on every collection/product page: collection-led `name` (§7.3 logic), `brand`, `material: "solid hardwood"`, description carrying the spec claims verbatim.
- **FAQPage** on the FAQ; **BreadcrumbList** sitewide.
- JSON-LD only — never microdata. Spanish pages carry the same schema with `inLanguage: "es"`.

## 11.3 llms.txt

Required at `clbailey.com/llms.txt`: the brand summary in the Module 3 voice, the §11.1 fact sheet, the collections list, the dealer-distribution model with the Find-a-Dealer link, and links to the guarantee, care guide, and FAQ. Reviewed quarterly alongside the §11.6 audit. The public site stays open to major AI crawlers in `robots.txt`; the dealer portal remains `noindex`/disallowed per §7.1 — proprietary pricing never feeds a model.

## 11.4 Claims Live in Crawlable Text

Every spec claim MUST exist as real HTML text on the page that makes it — never only inside an image, a render, or a PDF. The sanctioned "No MDF" contrast claim (§3.4) belongs on the About page *as text*. Alt-text law (§7.3) supports this; it does not replace it.

## 11.5 Bilingual Parity for Machines

- **hreflang pairs (`en`/`es` + `x-default`) on every page.** *Current violation: clbailey.com ships no hreflang tags — fix on site.*
- Spanish pages: identical §11.1 facts, locked 3-B translations, `inLanguage: "es"`.
- No untranslated UI strings on /es (see §3B.6 — currently violated in the top-level nav).

## 11.6 The Quarterly AI-Visibility Audit

Each quarter, ask the major assistants the money questions — "best solid hardwood pool table," "pool table that converts to a dining table," "heirloom game room furniture," and the same in Spanish — and record: Is the brand cited? Which claims get repeated? Which facts come back wrong? A wrong fact always traces to an inconsistent source; fix the source, not the symptom. Results file to the vault (`08_AI_Prompts/CLB-M11-Audit-Full-vNN.md`).

## APPENDIX B — THE ONE-PAGE DEALER CHEAT SHEET

1. Lead with **game room furniture**, not "pool table."
2. **Solid hardwood only.** Never MDF — use it only in the sanctioned contrast claim.
3. **1-inch diamond-honed slate. 8 hand-applied finishes.** Say the numbers.
4. CTA is **"Find a Dealer,"** never "Shop Now."
5. **MSRP only** in brand/co-op ads. No strikethrough pricing.
6. **Warm White 60 / Charcoal+Photography 30 / Sand+Bronze 10.** Bronze ≤ 5%, never text on light grounds. Heritage Green only in the mark and the felt.
7. **Oxford comma. One exclamation point, max.**
8. Co-brand: **parity lockup, Sand divider, shared baseline, never alter our mark.**
9. Photos: **warm, lived-in, families using it.** Never clinical white-background stock.
10. En español: trato de **usted**, traducciones bloqueadas del Módulo 3-B, colecciones nunca traducidas.
11. When in doubt, ask: *would this still look right in this home in 2055?*
